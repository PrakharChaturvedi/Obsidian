## Reinforcement learning:
 - Reinforcement learning works on a feedback-based process, in which an AI agent (A software component) automatically explore its surrounding by hitting & trail, taking action, learning from experiences, and improving its performance.
- Agent gets rewarded for each of good action and get punishment for each bad action. Hence the goal of reinforcement learning agent is to maximize the rewards.

- Components :
	- Aside from the agent and the environment, a reinforcement learning model has four essential components: 
		- <font color="#ffc000">Policy:</font> A policy determines how an agent behaves at a specific point in time. 
		- <font color="#ffc000">Reward:</font> A reward defines the goal of an RL issue. At each time step, the agent’s behaviors result in a reward. The ultimate purpose of the agent is to optimize the overall reward earned. 
		- <font color="#ffc000">Value function: </font> A state’s value is the total accumulated quantity of prizes that the agent may anticipate receiving in the future if it begins in that condition.
			- Values represent the long-term attractiveness of a collection of states based on the expected future states and the benefits produced by those states.
			- Even though a state produces a modest immediate reward, it might still be valuable since it is frequently followed by additional states that produce bigger benefits.
		- <font color="#ffc000">Environment model:</font> This is a mechanism that mimics environmental behavior and enables predictions about how the environment will respond. This model will let the agent forecast the next reward if an action is taken, enabling the agent to rely on developing action on future environmental responses.
- Types of approaches: 
	- <font color="#00b0f0">Value based</font> : The goal of a value-based Reinforcement Learning approach is **to optimize a value function V.** **(s)**. In this strategy, the agent anticipates a long-term return of the current policy states.
	- <font color="#00b0f0">Policy-based</font> : A policy-based approach is a type of method used to find an optimal policy for an agent to maximize its cumulative reward in an environment.
- Policy and Off-policy agent
	- <font color="#ffc000">Policy agent:</font> A policy agent is an agent in reinforcement learning that follows a specific strategy or policy to make decisions. The policy defines the mapping from states to actions, representing the agent's behavior in the environment.
	- <font color="#ffc000">Off-policy agent:</font> An off-policy agent is an agent that learns from a different policy than the one it uses to make decisions. In other words, it can learn from experiences generated by a different, possibly older, or random policy.

## Credit assignment system
- The credit assignment problem in reinforcement learning refers to the challenge of attributing the obtained reward to the actions that led to it. 
- It involves determining how much credit or responsibility each action taken by an agent deserves for the overall outcome, especially in situations where delayed rewards make it non-trivial to associate consequences with specific actions.
- Solving the credit assignment problem is crucial for an agent to effectively learn and improve its decision-making in complex environments.
## Model based v/s Model free reinforcement learning
- <font color="#92d050">Model-Based Reinforcement Learning:</font>
	- Overview: In a model-based approach, the agent builds an internal model or representation of the environment. This model is used to simulate the possible outcomes of different actions, allowing the agent to plan and make decisions based on this knowledge.
	- Pros:
		- **Model Accuracy:** The success of model-based RL heavily relies on the accuracy of the internal model. If the model is inaccurate, the decisions made by the agent may be suboptimal.
		- **Computational Complexity:** Building and maintaining an accurate model can be computationally expensive, especially for complex environments. 
	- Cons:
		- **Model Accuracy:** The success of model-based RL heavily relies on the accuracy of the internal model. If the model is inaccurate, the decisions made by the agent may be suboptimal.
		- **Computational Complexity:** Building and maintaining an accurate model can be computationally expensive, especially for complex environments. 

- <font color="#92d050">Model Free Reinforcement Learning:</font>
	- Overview: In a model-free approach, the agent directly learns a policy or a value function without explicitly building an internal model of the environment. The agent interacts with the environment, observes the outcomes of its actions, and adjusts its strategy based on the observed rewards.
	- Pros:
	    - **Simplicity:** Model-free methods are often simpler to implement and require fewer assumptions about the environment.
	    - **Robustness:** Model-free approaches can be more robust in situations where the environment is complex, dynamic, or hard to model accurately.
	- Cons:
	    - **Sample Inefficiency:** Model-free methods may require more samples to learn an effective policy, especially in complex environments where exploration is challenging.
	    - **Limited Planning:** Since model-free methods don't have an explicit model, they may struggle with tasks that require long-term planning.

- <font color="#92d050">Choosing Between Model-Based and Model-Free:</font>
	- The choice between model-based and model-free RL depends on the characteristics of the problem at hand.
	- Model-based methods may be preferred when sample efficiency is crucial, and an accurate model can be obtained.
	- Model-free methods may be more suitable for complex and dynamic environments where modeling is challenging, and robustness is a priority.

## Markov Decision Process :
- Definition : 
	- The Markov decision process (MDP) is **a mathematical framework used for modeling decision-making problems where the outcomes are partly random and partly controllable**. It's a framework that can address most reinforcement learning (RL) problems.
	- MDPs evaluate which actions the decision maker should take considering the current state and environment of the system.
- Working : 
	- **MDP Components:**
		- <font color="#ffc000">Agent</font>: Decision-making entity responsible for taking actions.
		- <font color="#ffc000">States</font>: Different situations or conditions in which the agent can find itself.
		- <font color="#ffc000">Actions</font>: Choices or decisions made by the agent in a given state.
		- <font color="#ffc000">Rewards</font>: Feedback or numerical values received by the agent based on its actions and the current state.
		- <font color="#ffc000">Optimal Policies</font>: Strategies or rules that guide the agent's actions to maximize cumulative rewards._![[Pasted image 20231211114711.png]]
	- **Agent-Environment Interaction:** 
		- The agent operates within an environment defined by various states.
		- Transitions occur as the agent moves from one state to another based on its actions.
	- **Transition Mechanism:**
		- MDP outlines how specific actions in certain states lead to transitions to other states.
	- **Reward System:**
		- The agent receives rewards corresponding to its actions and the current state.
		- Rewards serve as feedback to reinforce or discourage certain behaviors.
	- **Policy Definition:**
		- Policy dictates the agent's next action based on its current state.
		- It represents a strategy for decision-making in various situations.
- Summary :
	- The MDP model involves an agent making decisions in an environment characterized by states. Actions by the agent lead to transitions between states, with associated rewards influencing the agent's behavior. The policy guides the agent in determining its next action based on the current state, and the overall goal is to find optimal policies that maximize cumulative rewards over time.

## Markov chain
- A Markov Chain is a mathematical model that describes a system in which an entity transitions from one state to another over a sequence of discrete time steps. 
- The key characteristic of a Markov Chain is the Markov property, which states that the future state of the system depends only on its current state and is independent of how it reached that state. 
- Markov Chains are widely used in various fields, including physics, economics, biology, and computer science, to model and analyze systems with probabilistic transitions between states. They provide a simple yet powerful framework for understanding and predicting the behavior of stochastic processes.

## Association Rule Mining :
- Association rule mining is a data mining technique that identifies interesting relationships or associations among a set of variables in large datasets. It involves discovering patterns such as "if A, then B" or "A implies B" to reveal connections and dependencies between different items or events.
- 2 algorithms : 
	- FP Growth algorithm
	- Apriori algorithm
## FP Growth Algorithm
- The FP-Growth Algorithm is an alternative way to find frequent item sets without using candidate generations, thus improving performance. For so much, it uses a **divide-and-conquer strategy.** 
- The core of this method is the usage of a special data structure named frequent-pattern tree (FP-tree), which retains the item set association information.
- Algorithm:
	- First, it compresses the input database creating an FP-tree instance to represent frequent items.
	- After this first step, it divides the compressed database into a set of conditional databases, each associated with one frequent pattern.
	- Finally, each such database is mined separately.
- **FP tree** : 
	- Youtube link: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GzRi6pbdQ2E]
	- The frequent-pattern tree (FP-tree) is a compact data structure that stores quantitative information about frequent patterns in a database. Each transaction is read and then mapped onto a path in the FP-tree. 
	- This is done until all transactions have been read. Different transactions with common subsets allow the tree to remain compact because their paths overlap.

## Machine learning for disease Prediction
- Machine learning plays a pivotal role in predicting diseases by leveraging data-driven approaches to analyze patterns and make informed prognoses. 
- The process begins with <font color="#00b050">the collection of diverse datasets</font> encompassing patient demographics, medical histories, genetic profiles, lifestyle habits, and relevant health indicators. This data undergoes meticulous preprocessing to address missing values and outliers, ensuring its quality and reliability. 
- <font color="#00b050">Feature selection</font> identifies the most influential variables crucial for prediction tasks, aiding in streamlining models. 
- <font color="#00b050">Choosing an appropriate machine learning model</font>—such as logistic regression, decision trees, support vector machines, or neural networks—follows, with training performed on labeled datasets associating features with known disease outcomes. 
- <font color="#00b050"> Rigorous validation and fine-tuning of model parameters</font> optimize accuracy and performance metrics. The model's efficacy is tested on independent datasets to ensure its applicability to new, unseen data. 
- Deploying the trained model into real-world scenarios facilitates its usage for predicting diseases, thereby assisting healthcare professionals in early diagnosis, risk assessment, and personalized treatment strategies.
- *Continuous monitoring and updates sustain the model's relevance, ensuring it evolves with new data and technological advancements.*
- Example : Disease prediction using machine learning include predicting the risk of heart disease, diabetes, cancer, and various other medical conditions. 
	- The specific approach and models used can vary depending on the disease and the available data. 
	- Machine learning in healthcare has the potential to assist healthcare professionals in early diagnosis, risk stratification, and personalized treatment plans.
